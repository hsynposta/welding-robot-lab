Q1 · For θ₁(t)=0.02t²+0.2t, find θ̇₁ and θ̈₁ at t = 5 s.
θ̇₁ = 0.04t + 0.2 → 0.04·5 + 0.2 = 0.40 rad/s · θ̈₁ = 0.04 rad/s² (constant).
Q2 · Where is the tip at t = 5 s in Scenario 1 (l = 1.2 m)?
θ₁(5)=0.02·25+0.2·5=1.50 rad → X = 1.2·cos 1.5 = 0.08 m, Y = 1.2·sin 1.5 = 1.20 m.
Q3 · Compute the torque for Scenario 2 at t = 5 s (m=250, l=1.2, C=100).
θ₂=1.50 rad, θ̇₂=0.80 rad/s, θ̈₂=0.30 rad/s².
inertia m·l²·θ̈ = 360·0.30 = 108 · damping C·θ̇ = 80 · gravity (m·g·l/2)·cosθ = 1471.5·cos 1.5 = 104
Q ≈ 108 + 80 + 104 = 292 Nm · P = Q·θ̇ ≈ 234 W.
Q4 · Which scenario needs the bigger motor, and why?
Scenario 2 — its peak power is 595 W vs 377 W, because the cubic profile keeps accelerating (θ̈ = 0.06t grows) while the velocity is also at its largest. Motor sizing uses peak torque, peak speed and peak power together.
Q5 · Why can a constant torque never hold the arm steadily above the horizontal? (Module 04)
Above the datum, the equilibrium Q = (m·g·l/2)·cosθ is unstable: a tiny disturbance grows because gravity's restoring slope has the wrong sign. The stable equilibrium sits below the horizontal at θ = −arccos(2Q/(m·g·l)). Holding a pose above the datum needs feedback control — exactly what Module 06 (PID) adds.
